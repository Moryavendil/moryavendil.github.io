publication: '*The Journal of the Acoustical Society of America*'
doi: 10.1121/1.397353
abstract: 'An experimental study of the underwater sound produced by water drop impacts
  on the surface is described. It is found that sound may be produced in two ways:
  first when the drop strikes the surface and, second, when a bubble is created in
  the water. The first process occurs for every drop; the second occurs for some impacts
  but not others. A range of conditions is described in which a bubble is produced
  for every drop impact, and it is shown that these conditions are likely to be met
  by a significant fraction of the raindrops in a typical shower. Underwater sound
  produced by artificial as well as real rain is reported. A comparison between artificial
  and real rain noise power spectra shows some deviations due to different drop‐size
  distributions. Addition of surface tension reducing liquids to the water in the
  test tank caused a disappearance of the characteristic spectral peaks in the frequency
  range 14–16 kHz. These peaks have been observed by several scientists during measurements
  of real rain. Our findings provide evidence for the theory that the 14‐ to 16‐kHz
  spectral peak is caused by the ringing of bubbles entrained in the water by the
  drop impact process.'
